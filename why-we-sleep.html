Why We Sleep

By Matthew Walker, PhD

In his ode to sleep, Walker explains what scientists know about sleep - what it is, why we do it, and why it's so important for us to make it a priority.

About the Author

Matthew Walker, PhD, has studied sleep for a long time. He is currently a Professor of Neuroscience and Psychology at UC Berkeley. He is a founder of the Center for Human Sleep Science and has many other accomplishments in the neuroscience reasearch space.

Check out the author's website

Favorite Ideas

  • Sleep is likely an evolutionary trait that supports our brains in creativity, intelligence, and emotional regulation.
  • Whether you are a morning lark or a night owl, you have a natural circadian rhythm that determines your sleep cycle.
  • Sleep consists of 90 minute cycles of NREM and REM sleep.
  • During NREM sleep, brain waves move in unison from the front of the brain to the back, moving memories from short term to long term storage.
  • During REM sleep, the brain paralyzes the body and dreams occur
  • There is not a clear detemining factor for the amount of sleep a specific species needs.
  • Some animals dream, and some sleep with half their brain.
  • REM sleep is considered an evolutionary benefit that made humans the dominant species.
  • Babies are sleep nearly the entire time they are in the womb.
  • REM sleep is critical for development in the third trimester and for a while after birth.
  • Children and elderly people have early circadian rhythms, while teenagers have theirs shifted forward much later.
  • A lack of sleep may cause psychological disorders in teenagers and young adults, and dementia, alzheimers, among other illnesses in elders.
  • Adequate sleep provides proper management of short and long term memory, and improves learning outcomes.
  • Every hour, someone dies in a car accident resulting from lack of sleep.
  • Sleep deprived people are not aware of their impaired performance, which declines to that of a drunk driver after 19 hours awake.
  • Sleep deficiency increases your chances of heart attack by 200%. It is a leading cause of type 2 diabetes.
  • Reduced sleep weakens the immune system and causes faster aging.
  • During dreaming, the visual, motor, emotional, and autobiographical portions of the brain become active. The prefrontal cortex becomes inactive and the brain paralyzes the body.
  • Dreams heal emotional difficulties and solve problems.
  • Lucid dreamers can control their dreams.
  • Sleepwalking is a rare and usually harmless condition.
  • Emotional worry or stress can cause insomnia.
  • Rats die after approximately 20 days with no sleep.
  • Humans need about 7-9 hours of sleep each night.
  • While advancements in electricity and technology are beneficial for many reasons, innovations have negatively impacted sleep.
  • A bedside lamp is powerful enough to alter your brain's sleep signals.
  • Alcohol significantly impairs your sleep and dramatically decreases your memory abilities.
  • We are he only species that wakes up artificially.
  • Sleeping pills do not produce a natural sleep and have negative impacts on memory and health.
  • Sleep deprivation erodes productivity in office workers and produces the 3rd leading cause of death - medical errors.
  • Students perform on average 200 SAT pints better when high school start times are pushed forward 1 hour.
  • Advancements in smart technology, like AI powered thermostats and lights, can be used to promote sleep.
  • Schools are not teaching the benefits of getting enough sleep, and corporations often discourage sleep.
  • Sleeping in a hospital is difficult yet critical for patients, especially premature babies.
